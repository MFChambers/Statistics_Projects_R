# Load the food dataset
food <- read.csv("/Users/maiafaith/Downloads/Statistics-Notebook-master/food.csv")

food_clean <- food %>%
  filter(!is.na(GPA), !is.na(breakfast)) %>%
  mutate(
    GPA = as.numeric(GPA),
    breakfast = as.factor(breakfast),
    gender = as.factor(gender)
  )

Background

The study evaluates the impact of breakfast consumption (independent variable) on GPA (dependent variable) among 54 college students. The goal is to determine whether breakfast habits influence academic performance.

Hypotheses

ANOVA Null Hypothesis (0): All group means of GPA across breakfast consumption levels are equal.

Alternative Hypothesis (): At least one of the group means of GPA is different.

Kruskal-Wallis Null Hypothesis (0): The median GPA is the same across breakfast consumption groups.

Alternative Hypothesis (): The median GPA differs across breakfast consumption groups.

ANOVA

anova_model <- aov(GPA ~ breakfast, data = food_clean)
summary(anova_model)
##              Df Sum Sq Mean Sq F value Pr(>F)
## breakfast     1  0.044 0.04375   0.286  0.594
## Residuals   119 18.208 0.15301               
## 2 observations deleted due to missingness

Interpretation: The ANOVA results suggest no significant effect of breakfast consumption on GPA (=0.594).

Check ANOVA Assumptions

# Normality Check
qqnorm(resid(anova_model))
qqline(resid(anova_model), col = "purple")

# Homogeneity of Variances
leveneTest(GPA ~ breakfast, data = food_clean, center = "median")
## Levene's Test for Homogeneity of Variance (center = "median")
##        Df F value Pr(>F)
## group   1  0.3828 0.5373
##       119

Homogeneity of Variance: The test suggests that the variances of GPA across breakfast groups are approximately equal, meeting the assumption for conducting ANOVA. This means we can proceed with the one-way ANOVA analysis without concerns about violating the homogeneity of variance assumption.

Kruskal Wallis Test

kruskal_result <- kruskal.test(GPA ~ breakfast, data = food_clean)
print(kruskal_result)
## 
##  Kruskal-Wallis rank sum test
## 
## data:  GPA by breakfast
## Kruskal-Wallis chi-squared = 0.49068, df = 1, p-value = 0.4836

The Kruskal-Wallis test corroborates the earlier ANOVA result, indicating that breakfast consumption does not have a statistically significant impact on GPA in this dataset.

Conclusion

Null Hypothesis (0): The median GPA is the same across the breakfast consumption groups.

Alternative Hypothesis (): The median GPA differs across the breakfast consumption groups. Since the p-value (0.4836) is greater than 0.05 (common significance level), we fail to reject the null hypothesis. This means:

The one-way ANOVA suggests no significant effect of breakfast consumption on GPA (p=0.594). The Kruskal-Wallis test corroborates this finding, showing no significant difference in GPA medians (p=0.4836).

There is no statistically significant difference in median GPA between students who ate breakfast and those who didnt.

For a future study, it may be beneficial to include other factors in the model like study time, sleep patterns. It would also be beneficial to increase the sample size.
